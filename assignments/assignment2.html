Assignment 2 Exploratory Data Analysis

Yishi Wang — yishi@mit.edu — 11.C85 Spring 2025

Subtheme: Zoning and Housing Supply

Data Overview

The primary dataset, "Single-Family Zoning vs. Other Housing Zoning with Census Info in Metro Boston Municipalities," is derived from MACP’s Zoning Atlas. It includes spatial and tabular data for 101 Metro Boston municipalities, indicating whether areas are zoned for single-family housing or other housing types and whether they fall under the MBTA Communities list.
Additionally, the dataset incorporates demographic data from the 2010 Census (population, racial composition) and 2014-2018 Census estimates (income levels).
Another key dataset, "Mapping Inequality: Redlining Data", sourced from 1935-1940 City Survey Files, provides historical context on redlining policies.

Data Limitation

  1. The zoning dataset and MBTA Communities list lack timestamps, making it unclear whether the data is up to date.
  2. Demographic data is over five years old and may not reflect current trends.
  3. Some municipalities appear twice in the dataset due to their mixed zoning status. However, demographic discrepancies exist between these duplicates, despite census data being from the same period. For consistency, I calculated the average values for analysis.
  4. Several municipalities lack complete demographic data, which could mislead interpretations. For example, some municipalities, such as Sharon, Everett, Burlington, Sudbury, Belmont, Chelsea, Waltham, Framingham, Lynn, and Somerville, are recorded as having zero population, which is clearly incorrect.
  5. These errors likely stem from census block groups not aligning perfectly with municipal boundaries, affecting data accuracy. Future analysis should incorporate updated census data to ensure more reliable findings.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What are the housing zoning classifications across the municipalities in Metro Boston?
  2. How do zoning classifications differ among communities that are subject to the MBTA Community Act?
  3. Do municipal demographics shape housing zoning, or does zoning influence demographics?
  4. How has historical housing policy, such as redlining, affected today's housing zoning classifications?

Discoveries & Insights

This analysis initially focused on understanding the current distribution of housing zoning classifications across Metro Boston municipalities. It then explored potential interplay factors that influence zoning patterns, such as demographic composition, household income, housing type preferences, and historical zoning policies.

I was triggered to see how the MBTA Community Act impacts housing zoning status, particularly in municipalities that still maintain a high percentage (over 80%) of single-family zoning.

The following investigates moved on to whether zoning classifications correlate with demographic characteristics, including population size, racial composition, and household types. The goal is to determine whether zoning policies are shaped by demographic preferences or if zoning itself influences demographic composition.

Since household type alone did not correlate to zoning classifications, I considered whether transportation access might play a role.

At this stage, I had examined multiple aspects of the dataset "Single-Family Zoning vs. Other Housing Zoning with Census Info in Metro Boston Municipalities" but found no strong evidence that demographic characteristics or economic status directly influence housing zoning classifications or vice versa. In an effort to uncover additional factors shaping zoning policies, I expanded my search to "Mapping Inequality: Redlining Data".

Summary

This study finds that more than half of Metro Boston municipalities have at least 60% of their land zoned for single-family housing, with a significant number exceeding 80%. Many of these municipalities are subject to the MBTA Community Act, highlighting potential areas for zoning reform. However, the analysis does not reveal strong correlations between zoning classifications and demographic characteristics such as race, income, or household composition. Additionally, historical redlining maps align closely with areas currently zoned for higher-density housing, suggesting that past housing policies may have influenced modern zoning patterns.

Future analyses should explore the relationship between zoning policies and housing value, affordability. It is particularly interesting to investigate whether single-family zoning contributes to rising property values and housing costs, or if other factors, such as transportation networks, school qualities, or other amenities play a more significant role. Additionally, incorporating data on property sales and production rate could provide insights into whether single-family zoning protects home values or merely perpetuates housing scarcity. The intended research could inform discussions on zoning reform and the effectiveness of policies aimed at increasing housing availability in Metro Boston.
